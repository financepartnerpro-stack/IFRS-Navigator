IFRS 1 First-time Adoption of International Financial Reporting Standards ▼
Provides guidance for entities adopting IFRS for the first time, specifying how to transition from previous GAAP.
Objective
Ensure that an entity's first IFRS financial statements contain high-quality information that is transparent and comparable over all periods presented, provide a suitable starting point for accounting under IFRS, and can be generated at a cost that does not exceed the benefits.
Key Requirements
  • Opening IFRS statement of financial position at the date of transition
  • Retrospective application of IFRS effective at the end of the first IFRS reporting period
  • Mandatory exceptions and optional exemptions from full retrospective application
  • Reconciliation disclosures from previous GAAP to IFRS for equity, comprehensive income, and cash flows
Effective Date
1 July 2004 (amended periodically)
IFRS 2 Share-based Payment ▼
Prescribes financial reporting for share-based payment transactions including equity-settled, cash-settled, and choice arrangements.
Objective
Specify financial reporting requirements for transactions in which an entity receives or acquires goods or services as consideration for equity instruments, or incurs liabilities based on the price of equity instruments.
Key Requirements
  • Equity-settled: Measure at grant-date fair value of equity instruments; recognise over vesting period
  • Cash-settled (SARs): Remeasure liability at fair value each reporting date until settlement
  • Choice transactions: Bifurcate if entity has the choice; recognise liability component first
  • Non-market vesting conditions: adjust expense; market conditions: bake into grant-date fair value
  • Modifications: recognise incremental fair value at modification date
Effective Date
1 January 2005
IFRS 3 Business Combinations ▼
Specifies the acquisition method for accounting for business combinations, including goodwill recognition and measurement.
Objective
Improve the relevance, reliability, and comparability of information about business combinations and their effects.
Key Requirements
  • Acquisition method: identify acquirer, determine acquisition date, recognise and measure identifiable assets/liabilities and NCI
  • Goodwill = consideration transferred + NCI + fair value of prior interest − net identifiable assets
  • Bargain purchase (negative goodwill): recognised in profit or loss
  • Contingent consideration measured at fair value at acquisition date
  • Transaction costs expensed (not capitalised)
Effective Date
1 July 2009 (revised)
IFRS 4 Insurance Contracts (Superseded by IFRS 17) ▼
Superseded by IFRS 17 effective 1 January 2023. IFRS 4 allowed diverse accounting practices; largely no longer applicable.
Status
IFRS 4 has been superseded by IFRS 17 for annual periods beginning on or after 1 January 2023. Entities should now apply IFRS 17 for all insurance contract accounting.
Historical Scope
Permitted entities to continue their existing accounting policies for insurance contracts while imposing limited improvements and disclosure requirements as an interim measure pending IFRS 17.
Status
Superseded — IFRS 17 applies from 1 Jan 2023
IFRS 5 Non-current Assets Held for Sale and Discontinued Operations ▼
Governs classification, measurement, and presentation of non-current assets (or disposal groups) held for sale and discontinued operations.
Key Requirements
  • Classify as held for sale when: available for immediate sale in present condition + sale is highly probable within 12 months
  • Measure at lower of carrying amount or fair value less costs to sell
  • Cease depreciation/amortisation on classification
  • Present as a single line item on the statement of financial position
  • Discontinued operations: separate P&L presentation; comparative restatement required
Effective Date
1 January 2005
IFRS 6 Exploration for and Evaluation of Mineral Resources ▼
Provides limited guidance on accounting for exploration and evaluation expenditures, pending a comprehensive standard.
Key Requirements
  • Entities may develop an accounting policy for E&E assets using the hierarchy in IAS 8
  • E&E assets classified as tangible or intangible based on nature
  • Apply impairment test when facts suggest carrying amount may exceed recoverable amount
  • Disclose: policies, amounts recognised, and impairment losses/reversals
Effective Date
1 January 2006
IFRS 7 Financial Instruments: Disclosures ▼
Requires disclosures that enable users to evaluate the significance of financial instruments and associated risks.
Key Disclosure Areas
  • Significance of financial instruments: carrying amounts by category, fair value hierarchy, interest income/expense
  • Credit risk: maximum exposure, credit quality, collateral, impairment analysis, concentration
  • Liquidity risk: maturity analysis of financial liabilities
  • Market risk: sensitivity analysis for each type of market risk
  • Transfer of financial assets; offsetting; hedge accounting disclosures
Effective Date
1 January 2007
IFRS 8 Operating Segments ▼
Requires entities to disclose information based on internal management reporting — the "management approach" to segment reporting.
Key Requirements
  • Identify operating segments based on internal reports reviewed by chief operating decision maker (CODM)
  • Aggregation criteria: similar economic characteristics and qualitative factors
  • Quantitative thresholds for reportable segments (10% of combined revenue/profit/assets)
  • Reconcile segment totals to financial statements
  • Entity-wide disclosures: revenues by product/service, geography, and major customer
Effective Date
1 January 2009
IFRS 9 Financial Instruments ▼
Covers classification and measurement of financial instruments, the expected credit loss (ECL) impairment model, and hedge accounting.
Three Pillars
  • Classification & Measurement: Amortised cost, FVOCI, or FVTPL based on business model and SPPI test
  • Impairment (ECL Model): 3-stage model — 12-month ECL → lifetime ECL (significant credit deterioration) → credit-impaired. Applies to loans, receivables, lease receivables, contract assets
  • Hedge Accounting: Fair value hedges, cash flow hedges, net investment hedges; closer alignment with risk management
Key Change from IAS 39
Replaced the incurred-loss model with forward-looking ECL, requiring earlier recognition of credit losses.
Effective Date
1 January 2018
IFRS 10 Consolidated Financial Statements ▼
Establishes principles for presenting consolidated financial statements, with a single control model for all entities.
Control Model
An investor controls an investee when it has: (1) power over the investee; (2) exposure/rights to variable returns; and (3) ability to use power to affect those returns. All three elements must be present simultaneously.
Key Requirements
  • Consolidate all subsidiaries (entities controlled by the parent)
  • Uniform accounting policies for like transactions
  • Non-controlling interests presented within equity
  • Investment entities exception: measure subsidiaries at FVTPL instead of consolidating
Effective Date
1 January 2013
IFRS 11 Joint Arrangements ▼
Classifies joint arrangements as joint operations or joint ventures, each with distinct accounting treatment.
Classification
  • Joint Operation: Parties have rights to assets and obligations for liabilities — recognise share of assets, liabilities, revenues, expenses
  • Joint Venture: Parties have rights to the net assets — apply equity method per IAS 28
Key Change
Eliminated the proportionate consolidation method previously available for jointly controlled entities under IAS 31.
Effective Date
1 January 2013
IFRS 12 Disclosure of Interests in Other Entities ▼
Requires comprehensive disclosures about interests in subsidiaries, joint arrangements, associates, and unconsolidated structured entities.
Key Disclosure Requirements
  • Significant judgements and assumptions in determining control/significant influence
  • Information about composition of the group; restrictions on assets/liabilities
  • Nature and risks of interests in unconsolidated structured entities
  • Summarised financial information for material subsidiaries and equity-method investees
  • NCI interests: nature, proportion, summarised financials, dividends paid
Effective Date
1 January 2013
IFRS 13 Fair Value Measurement ▼
Defines fair value, establishes a single IFRS framework for measuring fair value, and requires disclosures about fair value measurements.
Fair Value Definition
The price that would be received to sell an asset or paid to transfer a liability in an orderly transaction between market participants at the measurement date (exit price).
Fair Value Hierarchy
  • Level 1: Quoted prices in active markets for identical assets/liabilities
  • Level 2: Observable inputs other than Level 1 (comparable transactions, yield curves)
  • Level 3: Unobservable inputs (entity's own assumptions, DCF models)
Effective Date
1 January 2013
IFRS 14 Regulatory Deferral Accounts ▼
Interim standard permitting first-time IFRS adopters in rate-regulated industries to continue their previous GAAP accounting for regulatory deferral balances.
Scope
Only available to first-time IFRS adopters that recognised regulatory deferral account balances under their previous GAAP. Not available to entities already applying IFRS.
Effective Date
1 January 2016 (interim — superseded by Rate-regulated Activities project)
IFRS 15 Revenue from Contracts with Customers ▼
Single comprehensive revenue recognition model — the 5-step model — replacing IAS 18 and IAS 11.
Five-Step Model
  • Step 1: Identify the contract with a customer
  • Step 2: Identify the performance obligations in the contract
  • Step 3: Determine the transaction price (including variable consideration)
  • Step 4: Allocate the transaction price to performance obligations (relative standalone selling price)
  • Step 5: Recognise revenue when (or as) each performance obligation is satisfied
Over Time vs Point in Time
Revenue recognised over time if: customer simultaneously receives and consumes benefits; entity creates/enhances customer-controlled asset; OR no alternative use and right to payment for work to date.
Effective Date
1 January 2018
IFRS 16 Leases ▼
Requires lessees to recognise a right-of-use asset and lease liability for virtually all leases, eliminating the operating lease "off balance sheet" treatment.
Lessee Accounting
  • Recognise ROU asset (initially at cost = present value of lease payments + initial direct costs + make-good obligations) and lease liability
  • Subsequently: depreciate ROU asset; unwind lease liability using effective interest method
  • Exemptions: short-term leases (≤12 months) and low-value assets (e.g., <USD 5,000)
Lessor Accounting
Unchanged from IAS 17: classify as finance lease or operating lease based on risks/rewards transfer.
Effective Date
1 January 2019
IFRS 17 Insurance Contracts ▼
Comprehensive standard for insurance contracts — replaces IFRS 4. Uses current measurement of insurance liabilities and a building block approach.
Measurement Models
  • General Model (GMM): Fulfilment cash flows (PV of future cash flows + risk adjustment) + Contractual Service Margin (unearned profit)
  • Premium Allocation Approach (PAA): Simplified model for short-duration contracts (≤1 year or approximates GMM)
  • Variable Fee Approach (VFA): For direct participation contracts
Key Concepts
CSM represents unearned profit recognised over the coverage period. Experience adjustments, changes in assumptions, and investment returns are carefully disaggregated in the income statement.
Effective Date
1 January 2023
IFRS 18 Presentation and Disclosure in Financial Statements ▼
Replaces IAS 1. Introduces new structure for the income statement with defined categories and management-defined performance measures (MPMs).
New Income Statement Structure
  • Five defined categories: Operating; Investing; Financing — integral to main business; Financing — not integral; Income tax
  • Two new required subtotals: Operating profit and Profit before financing and income tax
Management Performance Measures (MPMs)
Entities must disclose MPMs used in public communications (e.g., adjusted EBITDA) with a labelled reconciliation to IFRS line items and explanations of adjustments.
Effective Date
1 January 2027 (early adoption permitted)
IFRS 19 Subsidiaries without Public Accountability: Disclosures ▼
Permits eligible subsidiaries to apply reduced disclosure requirements while still applying the full IFRS recognition and measurement requirements.
Scope
Available to subsidiaries that: (a) do not have public accountability; (b) have an ultimate or intermediate parent that produces publicly available IFRS consolidated financial statements; and (c) have explicit shareholder approval.
Key Relief
Reduced disclosures across many standards (e.g., IFRS 7, IFRS 13, IAS 19, IAS 36) — recognition and measurement remains full IFRS.
Effective Date
1 January 2027 (early adoption permitted)

IAS Standards

International Accounting Standards — the predecessor standards to IFRS, many still in force. Issued by the IASC (predecessor to IASB) and subsequently maintained and amended by the IASB.

IAS 1Presentation of Financial Statements (Superseded by IFRS 18 from 2027)▼
Sets overall requirements for financial statements including structure, minimum content, and overriding concepts.
Core Requirements
  • Complete set of financial statements: SFP, P&L/OCI, SOCE, SCF, notes
  • Fair presentation and going concern assessment
  • Accrual basis (except cash flow statement)
  • Materiality; aggregation; offsetting prohibition
  • Comparative information for at least one prior period
Status
Superseded by IFRS 18 — effective 1 Jan 2027
IAS 2Inventories▼
Prescribes accounting treatment for inventories, including cost determination and the lower of cost and NRV principle.
Key Requirements
  • Measure at lower of cost and net realisable value (NRV)
  • Cost includes purchase price, conversion costs, other costs to bring to present location/condition
  • Cost formulas: FIFO or weighted average (LIFO prohibited under IFRS)
  • Disclose: accounting policy, carrying amount by category, write-downs and reversals
Effective Date
1 January 2005
IAS 7Statement of Cash Flows▼
Requires entities to provide information about historical changes in cash and cash equivalents via a statement of cash flows.
Three Activity Categories
  • Operating: Principal revenue-producing activities — direct method (preferred) or indirect method
  • Investing: Acquisition/disposal of long-term assets and other investments
  • Financing: Changes in equity capital and borrowings
Effective Date
1 January 1994
IAS 8Accounting Policies, Changes in Accounting Estimates and Errors▼
Prescribes criteria for selecting and changing accounting policies, and the treatment of changes in estimates and corrections of errors.
Key Principles
  • Accounting policy changes: retrospective application (restate comparatives)
  • Changes in estimates: prospective application
  • Prior period errors: retrospective restatement (correction in earliest comparative period possible)
  • Hierarchy for policy selection: specific IFRS → analogous IFRS → IASB Framework → other bodies' guidance
Effective Date
1 January 2005
IAS 10Events after the Reporting Period▼
Distinguishes adjusting from non-adjusting events after the reporting period and prescribes disclosure requirements.
Key Distinction
  • Adjusting events: Provide evidence of conditions existing at reporting date → adjust financial statements
  • Non-adjusting events: Arise after the reporting date → disclose nature and estimated financial effect
  • Going concern assessment must consider post-period information
Effective Date
1 January 2005
IAS 12Income Taxes▼
Prescribes accounting for current and deferred income taxes using the balance sheet liability method.
Key Requirements
  • Deferred tax: temporary differences between carrying amount of assets/liabilities and their tax bases
  • Deferred tax liabilities (DTL): recognise for all taxable temporary differences (limited exceptions)
  • Deferred tax assets (DTA): recognise to the extent probable that taxable profit will be available
  • Measure using enacted/substantively enacted tax rates at reporting date
  • Pillar Two amendments: disclosure requirements for Pillar Two exposure (2023 temporary exception)
Effective Date
1 January 1998 (amended continuously)
IAS 16Property, Plant and Equipment▼
Prescribes accounting treatment for property, plant and equipment including recognition, measurement (cost or revaluation), and depreciation.
Two Measurement Models
  • Cost model: Carried at cost less accumulated depreciation and impairment losses
  • Revaluation model: Carried at revalued amount (fair value at date of revaluation) less subsequent depreciation and impairment
  • Revaluation surplus recognised in OCI; transferred to retained earnings on derecognition or use
Component Approach
Each significant part of an asset with a different useful life or depreciation method is depreciated separately.
Effective Date
1 January 2005
IAS 17Leases (Superseded by IFRS 16)▼
Superseded by IFRS 16. Distinguished finance leases from operating leases based on transfer of risks and rewards.
Status
Superseded by IFRS 16 — effective 1 Jan 2019
IAS 19Employee Benefits▼
Prescribes accounting and disclosure for employee benefits including short-term benefits, post-employment benefits, other long-term benefits, and termination benefits.
Defined Benefit Plans
  • Net defined benefit liability/asset = present value of DBO − fair value of plan assets
  • Current service cost and net interest recognised in P&L
  • Remeasurements (actuarial gains/losses; return on plan assets) recognised immediately in OCI — not recycled
  • Requires actuarial valuation using projected unit credit method
Defined Contribution Plans
Contributions recognised as an expense when employees render service. No further obligation beyond contributions.
Effective Date
1 January 2013 (revised)
IAS 20Accounting for Government Grants and Disclosure of Government Assistance▼
Prescribes accounting for, and disclosure of, government grants and assistance.
Key Requirements
  • Grants not recognised until there is reasonable assurance of compliance and receipt
  • Grants related to assets: deduct from asset (net presentation) OR deferred income recognised over asset life
  • Grants related to income: recognised in P&L on a systematic basis matching related costs
  • Non-monetary grants: measured at fair value (or nominal value permitted)
Effective Date
1 January 1984 (amended)
IAS 21The Effects of Changes in Foreign Exchange Rates▼
Prescribes how to include foreign currency transactions and foreign operations in financial statements, and how to translate financial statements into a presentation currency.
Key Concepts
  • Functional currency: Currency of the primary economic environment; determined by specific indicators
  • Monetary items retranslated at closing rate; non-monetary items at historical rate (cost) or closing rate (fair value)
  • Exchange differences: P&L (monetary items) or OCI (net investment in foreign operations — FCTR)
  • Translation to presentation currency: assets/liabilities at closing rate; income/expenses at transaction date rate (or average)
Effective Date
1 January 2005
IAS 23Borrowing Costs▼
Requires capitalisation of borrowing costs attributable to the acquisition, construction, or production of a qualifying asset.
Key Requirements
  • Capitalise borrowing costs directly attributable to a qualifying asset (one that necessarily takes a substantial period of time to get ready)
  • Specific borrowings: actual costs less investment income on temporary reinvestment
  • General borrowings: capitalisation rate × average carrying amount of qualifying asset
  • Capitalisation commences when: expenditures incurred + borrowing costs incurred + activities in progress
Effective Date
1 January 2009
IAS 24Related Party Disclosures▼
Requires disclosures to draw attention to the possibility that financial position and results may have been affected by related party transactions.
Related Party Definition
Broad definition encompassing: parent; subsidiaries; associates; joint ventures; key management personnel (KMP); close family members of KMP; entities controlled/jointly controlled by KMP or close family; post-employment benefit plans.
Required Disclosures
Nature of relationship; description and amount of transactions; outstanding balances (including terms and conditions, guarantees); provisions for doubtful debts; KMP compensation by category.
Effective Date
1 January 2011
IAS 27Separate Financial Statements▼
Prescribes accounting for investments in subsidiaries, joint ventures, and associates in an entity's separate (non-consolidated) financial statements.
Measurement Options
Investments may be carried at: (a) cost; (b) in accordance with IFRS 9; or (c) using equity method (as described in IAS 28). Policy applied consistently per category of investment.
Effective Date
1 January 2013
IAS 28Investments in Associates and Joint Ventures▼
Prescribes the equity method of accounting for investments in associates and joint ventures.
Equity Method
  • Initially recognised at cost; subsequently adjusted for investor's share of post-acquisition profit/loss and OCI
  • Significant influence: presumed at ≥20% voting power (rebuttable)
  • Impairment assessed per IAS 36 (whole investment as a single asset)
  • Discontinued when significant influence is lost
Effective Date
1 January 2013
IAS 29Financial Reporting in Hyperinflationary Economies▼
Prescribes restatement of financial statements in the functional currency of a hyperinflationary economy using a general price index.
Hyperinflation Indicators
Cumulative inflation over 3 years approaching or exceeding 100%; population prefers non-monetary assets or stable foreign currency; interest rates, wages, prices reflect price index; sales prices quoted in stable foreign currency.
Restatement Approach
Restate non-monetary items using change in general price index; monetary items not restated; gain/loss on net monetary position recognised in P&L.
Effective Date
1 January 1990
IAS 32Financial Instruments: Presentation▼
Establishes principles for presenting financial instruments as liabilities or equity, and for offsetting financial assets and liabilities.
Liability vs Equity
A financial instrument is equity only if the issuer has no contractual obligation to deliver cash/financial assets, and (if settled in own equity instruments) it will be settled by a fixed amount of cash for a fixed number of equity instruments (fixed-for-fixed).
Offsetting
Offset only when: legally enforceable right to set off AND intention to settle net or simultaneously.
Effective Date
1 January 2005
IAS 33Earnings per Share▼
Prescribes principles for the determination and presentation of basic and diluted EPS for listed entities.
Calculations
  • Basic EPS: Profit or loss attributable to ordinary shareholders ÷ weighted average ordinary shares outstanding
  • Diluted EPS: Adjust numerator and denominator for dilutive potential ordinary shares (options, warrants, convertibles)
  • Retrospective adjustment for bonus issues, stock splits, share consolidations
Effective Date
1 January 2005
IAS 34Interim Financial Reporting▼
Prescribes minimum content of interim financial reports and principles for recognition and measurement in interim periods.
Key Requirements
  • Condensed or complete financial statements for interim periods
  • Discrete approach for most items; some items estimated on annual basis (income taxes)
  • Comparative periods specified for each financial statement
  • Significant events and transactions since last annual report: disclose in notes
Effective Date
1 January 1999
IAS 36Impairment of Assets▼
Ensures assets are not carried above their recoverable amount, and prescribes impairment testing including for goodwill and intangibles with indefinite life.
Recoverable Amount
Higher of: (a) Fair value less costs of disposal (FVLCD); and (b) Value in use (VIU) — present value of expected future cash flows from the asset or CGU.
CGU & Goodwill
Goodwill allocated to Cash Generating Units (CGUs) or groups; tested annually for impairment (not only when indicators exist). Impairment losses: first reduce goodwill; then pro-rata across other assets. Reversal of goodwill impairment prohibited.
Effective Date
31 March 2004
IAS 37Provisions, Contingent Liabilities and Contingent Assets▼
Establishes recognition and measurement criteria for provisions, and disclosure requirements for contingent items.
Recognition Criteria (Provisions)
  • Present obligation (legal or constructive) as a result of past event
  • Probable outflow of resources (more likely than not)
  • Reliable estimate of the obligation
Measurement
Best estimate of expenditure required to settle obligation; discount to present value if material; use expected value for large populations; most likely outcome for single obligation.
Effective Date
1 July 1999
IAS 38Intangible Assets▼
Prescribes recognition and measurement of intangible assets not covered by other standards, including internal development costs.
Recognition
Identifiable non-monetary asset without physical substance. Recognise when: probable future economic benefits AND cost can be reliably measured. Internally generated goodwill and brands: not recognised.
Research vs Development
Research costs: always expense. Development costs: capitalise when 6 specific criteria all met (technical feasibility, intention, ability to use/sell, probable economic benefits, adequate resources, reliable measurement).
Effective Date
31 March 2004
IAS 40Investment Property▼
Prescribes accounting for property held to earn rentals or for capital appreciation (or both), separately from owner-occupied property.
Two Measurement Models
  • Fair value model: Remeasure at fair value each reporting date; gains/losses to P&L; no depreciation
  • Cost model: Cost less accumulated depreciation and impairment (same as IAS 16); disclose fair value in notes
  • Policy must be applied to all investment property (except in very limited circumstances)
Effective Date
1 January 2005
IAS 41Agriculture▼
Prescribes accounting for biological assets and agricultural produce at the point of harvest.
Key Requirements
  • Biological assets measured at fair value less costs to sell at each reporting date (unless FV not reliably measurable)
  • Changes in FV less costs to sell recognised in P&L
  • Agricultural produce at point of harvest: measured at fair value less costs to sell (cost for subsequent IAS 2 purposes)
  • Bearer plants: accounted for under IAS 16 (not IAS 41)
Effective Date
1 January 2003

IFRIC Interpretations

Interpretations issued by the IFRS Interpretations Committee (formerly IFRIC) to provide guidance on applying IFRS to specific issues not directly addressed by a standard.

⚠ Several IFRIC interpretations have been withdrawn. Active status is indicated. Verify with the IFRS Foundation.
No matching standards found.
IFRIC 1Changes in Existing Decommissioning, Restoration and Similar Liabilities▼
Addresses accounting for changes in the measurement of a decommissioning or similar liability that has been included in the cost of an asset.
Key Guidance
When the related asset is measured at cost: add/deduct change in liability to/from asset cost. If deduction exceeds carrying amount of asset, excess recognised immediately in P&L. If asset is measured at revaluation: changes treated as revaluation movements through OCI.
Effective Date
1 September 2004
IFRIC 2Members' Shares in Co-operative Entities and Similar Instruments▼
Addresses the classification of members' shares in co-operative entities as liability or equity under IAS 32.
Key Principle
Members' shares are equity if the entity has an unconditional right to refuse redemption. Otherwise classified as financial liability. Restriction on redemption (e.g. minimum capital requirements) may allow equity classification for some shares.
Effective Date
1 January 2005
IFRIC 5Rights to Interests arising from Decommissioning, Restoration and Environmental Rehabilitation Funds▼
Addresses how a contributor should account for its interest in a fund set up to provide for future decommissioning.
Key Guidance
Recognise obligation to pay decommissioning costs per IAS 37. Right to receive reimbursement from fund accounted for per IAS 39/IFRS 9 — not offset against liability. Apply IFRS 10/IAS 28/IAS 31 to determine if control or significant influence exists over fund.
Effective Date
1 January 2006
IFRIC 6Liabilities arising from Participating in a Specific Market — Waste Electrical and Electronic Equipment▼
Addresses when to recognise a liability for waste management under EU-type regulations for historical electrical equipment.
Key Guidance
Obligating event is participation in the market during the measurement period, not manufacture of equipment. Liability recognised when obligating event occurs (typically annually as participation is measured).
Effective Date
1 December 2005
IFRIC 7Applying the Restatement Approach under IAS 29▼
Provides guidance on applying IAS 29 when an economy first becomes hyperinflationary in a particular period.
Key Guidance
In the first period of hyperinflationary reporting: restate non-monetary assets and liabilities from dates of acquisition/incurrence; IAS 12 deferred tax items restated and then current-period income tax recognised.
Effective Date
1 March 2006
IFRIC 10Interim Financial Reporting and Impairment▼
Addresses the interaction between IAS 34 and IAS 36/IFRS 9 on reversal of impairments recognised at an interim date.
Key Guidance
Impairment losses recognised in interim periods for goodwill, or investments in equity instruments and financial assets at cost, must NOT be reversed at a subsequent balance sheet date. IAS 34 does not override IAS 36 and IFRS 9 on this point.
Effective Date
1 November 2006
IFRIC 12Service Concession Arrangements▼
Addresses the accounting by private-sector operators involved in the provision of public services via service concession arrangements (PPPs).
Two Models
  • Financial asset model: Operator has unconditional right to receive cash from grantor — recognise financial asset
  • Intangible asset model: Operator has right to charge users — recognise intangible asset
  • Revenue and costs recognised per IFRS 15; construction/upgrade accounted for separately from operations
Effective Date
1 January 2008
IFRIC 14IAS 19 — The Limit on a Defined Benefit Asset, Minimum Funding Requirements and their Interaction▼
Provides guidance on assessing the limit on the amount recognised as a defined benefit asset, and on the effect of minimum funding requirements (MFRs).
Key Guidance
Economic benefit from refund or reduction in future contributions. MFR payments that create a prepayment: asset recognised to extent it will result in future economic benefit. Additional liability required if MFR creates an obligation exceeding the surplus.
Effective Date
1 January 2008
IFRIC 16Hedges of a Net Investment in a Foreign Operation▼
Clarifies the nature of the hedged risk, which entity in a group can hold the hedging instrument, and how to determine the amount reclassified to P&L on disposal.
Key Clarifications
  • Hedged risk: foreign currency risk between functional currency of net investment and presentation currency of consolidated statements
  • Hedging instrument: can be held by any entity in the group (not only the parent)
  • On disposal: reclassify hedging gain/loss from FCTR to P&L (same as reclassification of FCTR itself)
Effective Date
1 October 2008
IFRIC 17Distributions of Non-cash Assets to Owners▼
Clarifies how to account for distributions of non-cash assets (dividends in kind) to owners.
Key Guidance
Dividend payable recognised at fair value of assets to be distributed. Difference between carrying amount of assets distributed and fair value: recognised in P&L at settlement. If subsidiary distributed: apply IFRS 10 deconsolidation guidance.
Effective Date
1 July 2009
IFRIC 19Extinguishing Financial Liabilities with Equity Instruments▼
Addresses accounting when an entity renegotiates the terms of a financial liability and issues equity instruments to the creditor to extinguish all or part of the liability.
Key Guidance
Equity instruments issued measured at fair value (unless FV cannot be reliably measured — then at FV of liability extinguished). Difference between carrying amount of liability and FV of equity instruments: recognised in P&L.
Effective Date
1 July 2010
IFRIC 20Stripping Costs in the Production Phase of a Surface Mine▼
Addresses the accounting for waste removal (stripping) costs incurred in open pit mining during the production phase.
Key Guidance
Stripping activity asset recognised when: identifiable component of ore body is improved; future economic benefits are probable; costs attributable to that component can be measured reliably. Measured initially at cost; subsequently at cost or revaluation (consistent with related ore body component).
Effective Date
1 January 2013
IFRIC 21Levies▼
Clarifies when to recognise a liability to pay a levy imposed by a government under IAS 37, specifically addressing the obligating event.
Key Guidance
Obligating event is the activity that triggers payment of the levy per the legislation. Liability recognised progressively (over the period) only if activity that triggers levy occurs over time. No liability recognised before the obligating event — not at the beginning of the annual period.
Effective Date
1 January 2014
IFRIC 22Foreign Currency Transactions and Advance Consideration▼
Addresses which exchange rate to use when recognising an asset, expense, or income when advance consideration is received/paid in a foreign currency.
Key Guidance
The date of the transaction (for the purpose of determining the exchange rate) is the date on which the entity initially recognises the non-monetary asset/liability arising from the advance consideration. Multiple advance payments: use exchange rate for each instalment separately.
Effective Date
1 January 2018

Clarifies how to apply the recognition and measurement requirements in IAS 12 when there is uncertainty over income tax treatments.
Key Guidance
  • Consider whether to treat uncertain tax treatments separately or together
  • Assume tax authority will examine and has full knowledge of all information
  • Measurement: most likely amount OR expected value (probability-weighted) — whichever better predicts resolution
  • Reassess when facts and circumstances change
Effective Date

Q 1When should revenue be recognised under IFRS 15?▼
Revenue is recognised when (or as) a performance obligation is satisfied — i.e., when control of a good or service transfers to the customer.
Explanation
IFRS 15 uses a five-step model: (1) identify the contract; (2) identify performance obligations; (3) determine the transaction price; (4) allocate the price to obligations; (5) recognise revenue when each obligation is satisfied. Revenue is recognised over time if the customer simultaneously receives and consumes benefits, or the entity has a right to payment for work completed to date with no alternative use. Otherwise, revenue is recognised at a point in time when control transfers.
Common Pitfall
Confusing delivery of goods with transfer of control. Control can transfer before or after physical delivery depending on contract terms (e.g., bill-and-hold arrangements, consignment stock).
Standard Reference
IFRS 15 — Revenue from Contracts with Customers (effective 1 January 2018)
Q 2What is the difference between a finance lease and an operating lease under IFRS 16?▼
Under IFRS 16, the lessee/lessor distinction still matters for lessors, but lessees must now recognise almost all leases on the balance sheet.
Explanation
For Lessees: IFRS 16 eliminated the operating/finance distinction for lessees. All leases (except short-term ≤12 months and low-value assets) are recognised as a Right-of-Use (ROU) asset and lease liability. The P&L shows depreciation on the ROU asset and interest on the lease liability — front-loaded compared to straight-line rent expense under the old IAS 17.

For Lessors: The finance/operating distinction remains. A finance lease transfers substantially all risks and rewards of ownership; the lessor derecognises the asset and records a receivable. An operating lease keeps the asset on the lessor's books; rental income is recognised on a straight-line basis.
Practical Impact
Lessees now show higher assets and liabilities, higher EBITDA (rent replaced by depreciation + interest), but lower profit in early years. DSCR and leverage ratios are commonly affected.
Standard Reference
IFRS 16 — Leases (effective 1 January 2019)
Q 3How is goodwill tested for impairment under IAS 36?▼
Goodwill is allocated to Cash Generating Units (CGUs) and tested for impairment annually — not amortised.
Explanation
Goodwill cannot be tested in isolation — it must be allocated to the smallest group of assets that generates largely independent cash flows (a CGU or group of CGUs). Each year, the carrying amount of the CGU (including allocated goodwill) is compared to its recoverable amount (the higher of Fair Value Less Costs of Disposal and Value in Use). If carrying amount exceeds recoverable amount, an impairment loss is recognised — first reducing goodwill, then other assets pro-rata.
Key Rules
  • Goodwill impairment is irreversible — once written down, cannot be reversed
  • Annual testing required even if no impairment indicators
  • Value in Use uses pre-tax cash flows discounted at a pre-tax rate
  • Sensitivity disclosures required for CGUs where reasonable change in assumption would cause impairment
Standard Reference
IAS 36 — Impairment of Assets; IFRS 3 — Business Combinations
Q 4What is the Expected Credit Loss (ECL) model under IFRS 9?▼
The ECL model requires entities to recognise credit losses before a default event occurs, based on forward-looking information.
The Three-Stage Model
  • Stage 1 (Performing): No significant increase in credit risk since origination → recognise 12-month ECL
  • Stage 2 (Underperforming): Significant increase in credit risk → recognise lifetime ECL (no interest income adjustment)
  • Stage 3 (Credit-impaired): Objective evidence of impairment → recognise lifetime ECL; interest income calculated on net carrying amount
Simplified Approach
For trade receivables, contract assets, and lease receivables without a significant financing component, entities may always use lifetime ECL (no staging required). A provision matrix (aging-based) is commonly used in practice.
Common Pitfall
ECL must incorporate forward-looking macroeconomic information (GDP growth, unemployment forecasts) — it is not simply a historical loss rate.
Standard Reference
IFRS 9 — Financial Instruments (effective 1 January 2018)
Q 5How are deferred tax assets and liabilities recognised under IAS 12?▼
Deferred tax arises from temporary differences between an asset or liability's carrying amount and its tax base.
Explanation
IAS 12 uses the balance sheet liability method. A Deferred Tax Liability (DTL) arises when the carrying amount of an asset exceeds its tax base (taxable temporary difference) — recognise in almost all cases. A Deferred Tax Asset (DTA) arises when the carrying amount of an asset is less than its tax base, or when tax losses can be carried forward — recognise only to the extent it is probable that future taxable profits will be available.
Key Exceptions (No Deferred Tax)
  • Initial recognition of goodwill
  • Initial recognition of asset/liability in a transaction that is not a business combination and affects neither accounting nor taxable profit at the time
  • Investments in subsidiaries/associates where the entity controls the timing of reversal and reversal is not probable in the foreseeable future (DTL only)
Measurement
Use enacted or substantively enacted tax rates at the reporting date expected to apply when temporary differences reverse. Do not discount deferred tax balances.
Standard Reference
IAS 12 — Income Taxes
Q 6When must an entity consolidate another entity under IFRS 10?▼
Consolidation is required when an investor controls an investee — based on a three-element control test, not just ownership percentage.
The Control Test
An investor controls an investee when it has ALL three of:
  • Power over the investee (existing rights that give current ability to direct relevant activities)
  • Exposure to variable returns from its involvement
  • Ability to use power to affect those variable returns
All three must exist simultaneously. Majority ownership (>50%) typically confers control but is not the only path.
Special Cases
  • De facto control: Possible with less than majority voting rights if other shareholders are widely dispersed
  • Investment entities: Exempt from consolidation — measure subsidiaries at FVTPL instead
  • Structured entities: May be controlled without equity ownership
Standard Reference
IFRS 10 — Consolidated Financial Statements; IFRS 12 — Disclosure of Interests in Other Entities
Q 7What is the difference between a provision and a contingent liability under IAS 37?▼
A provision is recognised on the balance sheet; a contingent liability is only disclosed in notes (or not disclosed if remote).
Provision — Recognise When
  • Present obligation (legal or constructive) from a past event
  • Probable outflow of economic resources (more likely than not — generally >50%)
  • Reliable estimate can be made
Contingent Liability — Disclose When
A possible obligation whose existence depends on future uncertain events outside the entity's control; OR a present obligation where outflow is not probable OR amount cannot be reliably measured. Disclose in notes unless the possibility of outflow is remote (no disclosure needed if remote).
Common Pitfall
Management's intent to contest a claim does not eliminate the obligation. If a court judgment or regulatory finding has created a present obligation, a provision is likely required even if an appeal is lodged.
Standard Reference
IAS 37 — Provisions, Contingent Liabilities and Contingent Assets
Q 8How are property, plant and equipment measured after initial recognition under IAS 16?▼
Entities choose either the cost model or the revaluation model — applied consistently to each class of PPE.
Cost Model
Carried at cost less accumulated depreciation and impairment losses. Simpler to apply; no need for regular valuations.
Revaluation Model
Carried at fair value at revaluation date, less subsequent depreciation and impairment. Revaluations must be regular enough so that carrying amount does not differ materially from fair value. Upward revaluations go to OCI (revaluation surplus in equity); downward revaluations go to P&L (unless reversing a prior surplus).
Component Approach
Each significant component with a different useful life must be depreciated separately. For example, an aircraft's airframe, engines, and interior fittings may each have distinct useful lives.
Standard Reference
IAS 16 — Property, Plant and Equipment; IAS 36 — Impairment of Assets
Q 9How are financial instruments classified and measured under IFRS 9?▼
Classification depends on the entity's business model for managing financial assets and the contractual cash flow characteristics of the instrument.
Financial Assets — Three Categories
  • Amortised Cost: Business model is "hold to collect" AND cash flows are solely payments of principal and interest (SPPI). Examples: loans, trade receivables held to maturity.
  • Fair Value through OCI (FVOCI): Business model is "hold to collect and sell" AND SPPI test passed; OR equity instruments designated at FVOCI (gains/losses never recycled to P&L).
  • Fair Value through P&L (FVTPL): All others, including derivatives and trading portfolios. Also available as an irrevocable designation to eliminate accounting mismatch.
Financial Liabilities
Generally measured at amortised cost. Exceptions: liabilities held for trading, derivatives, and those designated at FVTPL. For FVTPL liabilities, own credit risk changes go to OCI (not P&L).
Standard Reference
IFRS 9 — Financial Instruments; IFRS 7 — Financial Instruments: Disclosures
Q 10What is the difference between functional currency and presentation currency?▼
Functional currency is the currency of the primary economic environment in which an entity operates; presentation currency is the currency chosen for reporting.
Functional Currency
Determined by facts — primarily the currency that influences sales prices, labour, and material costs. Cannot be freely chosen. Indicators include: currency of pricing, financing, and receipts retained. An entity may only have one functional currency.
Presentation Currency
An entity may present its financial statements in any currency. If presentation currency differs from functional currency, translate using the closing rate for assets/liabilities and average rate for income/expenses. Differences go to OCI as Foreign Currency Translation Reserve (FCTR).
Common Issue
Entities in dollarised economies or with USD-denominated revenues often face judgement about whether their functional currency is USD or local currency. The determination follows substance, not management preference.
Standard Reference
IAS 21 — The Effects of Changes in Foreign Exchange Rates
Q 11How should borrowing costs be treated for a qualifying asset?▼
Borrowing costs directly attributable to acquiring or constructing a qualifying asset must be capitalised — not expensed.
Qualifying Asset
An asset that necessarily takes a substantial period of time to get ready for its intended use or sale. Examples: self-constructed factory, major infrastructure project, bespoke vessel. Inventories manufactured in large quantities on a repetitive basis are generally not qualifying assets.
Capitalisation Rate
  • Specific borrowings: Actual interest cost less investment income earned on temporarily invested funds
  • General borrowings: Weighted average cost of general borrowings × average carrying amount of qualifying asset during the period
Capitalisation begins when: expenditures are incurred, borrowing costs are incurred, AND activities to prepare the asset are in progress. Suspend during extended interruptions; cease when substantially complete.
Standard Reference
IAS 23 — Borrowing Costs
Q 12What are the key steps for a first-time adopter of IFRS?▼
First-time adoption requires an opening IFRS balance sheet and retrospective application of IFRS effective at the end of the first IFRS reporting period.
Key Steps
  1. Determine the date of transition (beginning of earliest comparative period presented)
  2. Prepare an opening IFRS Statement of Financial Position at the transition date
  3. Apply all IFRS effective at the end of the first IFRS reporting period retrospectively (with mandatory exceptions and optional exemptions)
  4. Prepare reconciliation statements: equity at transition date, equity at end of comparative period, and total comprehensive income for the comparative period
Common Optional Exemptions
Deemed cost for PPE (use fair value or previous GAAP value as deemed cost); cumulative translation differences (reset FCTR to zero); share-based payment transactions; leases; business combinations before transition date.
Standard Reference
IFRS 1 — First-time Adoption of International Financial Reporting Standards
Q 13When can liabilities be classified as non-current?▼
A liability is non-current only if the entity has the right to defer settlement for at least 12 months after the reporting date — assessed at the reporting date itself.
Key Principle (Post-2024 Amendment)
Following amendments to IAS 1 (effective 1 January 2024), the classification depends solely on whether the right to defer exists at the end of the reporting period — not on management's intention or likelihood of exercise. If covenants are tested after the reporting date, the liability may still be non-current if no breach has occurred by reporting date, but enhanced disclosures are required about the risk of reclassification.
Practical Issue
Revolving credit facilities and term loans with financial covenants require careful analysis. If a breach at reporting date gives the lender the right to demand repayment, the debt must be classified as current — even if a waiver is obtained after the reporting date (a non-adjusting event).
Standard Reference
IAS 1 — Presentation of Financial Statements (amended 2023, effective 1 January 2024)
Q 14How is the equity method applied for associates under IAS 28?▼
Under the equity method, the investment is initially recorded at cost and adjusted for the investor's share of post-acquisition profits, losses, and OCI.
Significant Influence
Presumed when the investor holds 20%–50% of voting power (rebuttable). Other indicators: board representation, participation in policy-making, material intercompany transactions, interchange of personnel, provision of essential technical information.
Mechanics
  • Investment initially at cost (including transaction costs)
  • Adjust carrying amount for share of associate's profit/loss → P&L; and OCI → OCI
  • Dividends received reduce the carrying amount
  • Eliminate unrealised profits on upstream and downstream transactions (to the extent of investor's interest)
  • If share of losses exceeds carrying amount: reduce to zero; continue recognising losses only if entity has obligations
Standard Reference
IAS 28 — Investments in Associates and Joint Ventures
Q 15How are inventory write-downs and reversals handled under IAS 2?▼
Inventories are measured at the lower of cost and net realisable value (NRV). Write-downs are expensed; reversals are permitted if NRV subsequently recovers.
Net Realisable Value
The estimated selling price in the ordinary course of business less estimated costs of completion and estimated costs necessary to make the sale. Assessed item by item (or by groups of similar items for practical purposes).
Write-down and Reversal
Write inventory down to NRV when cost exceeds NRV — recognised as an expense in the period. If in a subsequent period the NRV increases (e.g., selling price recovers), the write-down may be reversed up to the original write-down amount. The reversal is recognised as a reduction in the cost of inventories expense.
Cost Formulas
IAS 2 permits: FIFO (First-In, First-Out) or Weighted Average Cost. LIFO is prohibited under IFRS. The same cost formula must be applied to all inventories of similar nature and use.
Standard Reference
IAS 2 — Inventories
Q 16What disclosures are required for related party transactions under IAS 24?▼
IAS 24 requires disclosure of related party relationships and transactions regardless of whether a price was charged, to highlight potential influence on financial results.
Who is a Related Party?
Parent; subsidiaries; associates; joint ventures; key management personnel (KMP) and their close family members; entities controlled or significantly influenced by KMP or their close family; post-employment benefit plans of the reporting entity.
Required Disclosures
  • Nature of the relationship
  • Description and amounts of transactions
  • Outstanding balances: terms, conditions, guarantees, settlement method
  • Provisions for doubtful debts related to outstanding balances
  • KMP compensation: short-term benefits, post-employment, termination, share-based payment — by category
Government-related Entities
Partial exemption from the detailed disclosure requirements for transactions with government-controlled entities, if certain conditions are met. General nature and significance still required.
Standard Reference
IAS 24 — Related Party Disclosures
Q 17What changes does IFRS 18 introduce to the income statement from 2027?▼
IFRS 18 (replacing IAS 1 from 1 January 2027) restructures the income statement into five defined categories and introduces new required subtotals.
Five Income Statement Categories
  1. Operating — core business activities
  2. Investing — returns from assets not integral to main business
  3. Financing — integral to main business (e.g., insurance investment returns for insurers)
  4. Financing — not integral — borrowing costs and financing activities
  5. Income tax
New Required Subtotals
  • Operating profit — explicitly defined for the first time in IFRS
  • Profit before financing and income tax — captures operating + investing categories
Management Performance Measures (MPMs)
Entities using non-IFRS measures (e.g., adjusted EBITDA, adjusted EPS) in public communications must disclose a labelled reconciliation to the closest IFRS line item, with explanations of each adjustment and the income tax and NCI effects.
Standard Reference
IFRS 18 — Presentation and Disclosure in Financial Statements (effective 1 January 2027; early adoption permitted). Verify current status at ifrs.org.
Q 18How should an entity account for defined benefit pension obligations under IAS 19?▼
Defined benefit obligations are measured using the Projected Unit Credit method; remeasurements go to OCI and are never recycled to profit or loss.
Measurement of the Net Obligation
Net Defined Benefit Liability (Asset) = Present Value of Defined Benefit Obligation (DBO) − Fair Value of Plan Assets. An actuarial valuation using the Projected Unit Credit method is required. The discount rate is determined by reference to high-quality corporate bond yields (or government bonds in markets with no deep corporate bond market).
P&L Components
  • Current service cost
  • Past service cost (recognised immediately on plan amendment/curtailment)
  • Net interest on net defined benefit liability/asset
OCI Components (Remeasurements — Not Recycled)
  • Actuarial gains and losses on the DBO
  • Return on plan assets (excluding net interest)
  • Effect of the asset ceiling (IFRIC 14)
Standard Reference
IAS 19 — Employee Benefits; IFRIC 14 — IAS 19: The Limit on a Defined Benefit Asset
Q 19What are the criteria for capitalising development costs as intangible assets under IAS 38?▼
Development costs are capitalised only when all six specified criteria are met; research costs are always expensed.
Research vs Development
Research phase: Original and planned investigation to gain new knowledge — always expense. Development phase: Application of research findings to a plan or design — capitalise IF all six criteria are met simultaneously.
The Six Criteria (All Must Be Met)
  1. Technical feasibility of completing the asset
  2. Management's intention to complete and use/sell the asset
  3. Ability to use or sell the asset
  4. The asset will generate probable future economic benefits
  5. Availability of adequate technical, financial, and other resources to complete the project
  6. Reliable measurement of expenditure attributable to the asset during development
Common Pitfall
Internally generated brands, mastheads, publishing titles, customer lists, and goodwill may never be capitalised. Internally generated computer software may qualify for capitalisation in the development phase.
Standard Reference
IAS 38 — Intangible Assets
Q 20How is fair value measured under IFRS 13?▼
Fair value is an exit price — the price received to sell an asset or paid to transfer a liability in an orderly transaction between market participants at the measurement date.
Key Concept: Exit Price
Fair value reflects a hypothetical transaction from the perspective of a market participant holding the asset or owing the liability — not an entity-specific value. The principal market (or most advantageous market) is used. Transaction costs are not included in fair value (but may affect the measurement of net realisable value under other standards).
The Fair Value Hierarchy
  • Level 1: Quoted prices in active markets for identical assets or liabilities — highest reliability; use without adjustment
  • Level 2: Observable inputs other than Level 1 — comparable asset prices, interest rate curves, credit spreads
  • Level 3: Unobservable inputs — entity's own assumptions about what market participants would use, including DCF models, earnings multiples. Highest judgement and disclosure requirements.
Valuation Techniques
Market approach (comparable transactions); Income approach (DCF, option pricing); Cost approach (replacement cost). Use the technique that maximises observable inputs. Consistency in technique selection is required; changes treated as a change in accounting estimate.
Standard Reference
IFRS 13 — Fair Value Measurement
